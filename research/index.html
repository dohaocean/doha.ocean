Overview:

4.5 billion years of geologic history --- from the formation of continental crust to the Wilson cycles of supercontinents, has shaped our planet Earth. Trained in Geophysics and general Earth sciences, I enjoy using Geodynamic models to study magmatic and tectonic processes at the Earth’s plate boundaries and during formation of Large Igneous Provinces.

Ongoing Research at Boston College (advisor: Prof. Mark Behn and Garrett Ito):

Magmatism Controls Global Oceanic Transform Fault Topograhpy

Under review (minor revision) at Nature Communications.-->link to preprint

Spreading-rate-dependent differences in magma supply controls global variations in oceanic transform fault topographies.

Global oceanic transform faults display an enigmatic continuum of morphologies ranging from deep valleys to small ridges. Three-dimensional numerical models that simulate two mid-ocean ridge segments separated by a transform fault show that magmatically accommodated extension within the transform domain exerts a first-order control on transform fault topography. Low rates of magmatism cause along-transform tectonic stretching, generating deep transform valleys and fracture zones. Intermediate rates of magmatism fully accommodate far-field stretching, but across-transform shear-induce tensile stress produce shallow transform valleys whose depth increases with shear strength of the transform. High rates of magmatism lead to curved plate boundaries and local compression, which generates elevated fault-parallel ridges. Thus, the entire spectrum of transform morphology can arise without requiring a change of plate motion. The models also generate shallower fracture zones than the adjacent transform valleys, consistent with global systematics.
